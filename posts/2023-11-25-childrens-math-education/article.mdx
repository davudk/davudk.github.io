I recently came across a video titled [This simple game makes kids better at math](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LFR3J9KQsNo)
by Johns Hopkins University.
In the video, the children play a simple game where each round they're presented with two bunches of circles.

Yellow and blue circles are placed on opposite sides of the screen.
The quantity, size and placement of the circles are randomized.

The circles are only visible for about 1-2 seconds before they disappear.
The child must the determine which side (yellow or blue) has more circles based on a quick glance.

![A child plays the game created by Johns Hopkins University.](2023-11-25-childrens-math-education/kid-plays-game.jpg)

When I saw this video, I knew I wanted to try this on my nephew.

Since my end goal is to improve the speed at which he can solve simple addition and subtraction problems,
I decided that I would:

1. First work to improve his counting speed by having him count randomized bunches of circles.
2. Then introduce a second batch of circles and have him determine which batch is greater in count.

I thought of adding other variables as well, such as a time limit or introducing other shapes which need to be ignored.
But after some thought I decided to keep it simple for now.

## Step 1: Just counting randomized bunches of circles

I prepared the following interactive demo to evaluate my nephew.
The demo below randomly generates an adjustable amount of circles each round.
Press `space` or click the `↻` button to reset.

<CircleCountingGame id="game-1" />

I had my nephew play this demo for about 15 minutes.
I would show him the circles, ask him how many there were, and then reset the board.

During our first session, I noticed a few things:

1. I had no easy way to quantifiably assess his ability to count, meaning that I wasn't able to acquire a baseline before
our session or quantify his progress by the end of the session.
2. Despite point #1, I saw significant progress in his ability to count.
At the start of our session he was counting 1) out loud and 2) by pointing at the circles with his index finger.
By the end, he was able to count 1) silently, 2) without pointing and 3) much faster (you have to take my word for it).
3. I also realized that when there were lots of circles (especially ones that overlapped), he would resort to guessing.
I learned to tell when he was guessing because he would say his answer with uncertainty in his voice
(e.g. a "six" that he counted was said much more confidently than a "six" that he guessed).
I was able to convince him to count rather than guess through positive reinforcement.
4. And lastly, during the session I began to doubt whether it was at all necessary to practice counting in order
to prepare for the actual experiment that was performed by JHU --determining which bunch (yellow or blue) has more circles.

Although I didn't have any specific metrics on hand, I decided that this was a still productive session.
